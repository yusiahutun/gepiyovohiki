20+ Funny Kikuyu proverbs and sayings with their meaning

Publish date: 2024-04-27

Kikuyu is one of the most widely spoken languages in central Kenya. The Kikuyu people are also among the numerous Bantu tribes in East Africa. Like other local Kenyan languages, Kikuyu has a rich heritage, with countless sayings, proverbs, and witty phrases. These are used as part of day-to-day conversations, often to drive a point home. These Kikuyu proverbs and sayings carry deep meanings behind them, with some being outright hilarious.

The sayings, phrases, and proverbs have been developed gradually over the numerous years the Kikuyu people have been in existence. Here is a look at some funny Kikuyu proverbs and their meanings.

Hilarious Kikuyu proverbs

These proverbs are derived from the witty use of some funny Kikuyu words combined with hilarious and often deep meanings.

  • Aka matirī cīa ndiiro no cīa nyanīko.

Translation: Wives do not have upright words; they only have embarrassing ones. The proverb was used to describe ancient Kikuyu wives' inability to keep secrets.

  • Gútirī ita rithiagwwo na gītete kīa njohī no gīa úcúrú.

Translation: There isn't a war where the warriors carry calabashes full of alcohol; they only carry those of porridge. The proverb was used to describe the inebriating attributes of alcohol, making it entirely useless for any helpful endeavours.

  • Mútirima wa kīrimú wītirimagia na múgī.

Translation: The walking stick belonging to a fool often benefits the wise. The proverb means that wise people know how to benefit from things that fools often deem useless.

  • Gútirī múrīo útainagīa rúthīa.

Translation: Every type of pleasure (however little) causes a person's cheeks to jiggle. The Kikuyu consider a jiggling of cheeks a sign of joy. The proverb means that no happiness is little.

  • Kīere kīa njīra-inī gītigwatagwo nī muura.

Translation: A grain of millet lying by the road will never get to bear any ears. This one was directed to promiscuous women, reminding them that their behaviour gives them little or no hope of ever getting children.

  • Gútirī mwana wītemaga agītemera ithe.

Translation: There is no son who cuts himself while cutting (meat) for his father. This is among the most popular proverbs in Kikuyu. It is used to describe the stinginess some children often portray towards their parents.

  • Hinga ndīkinyaga iraka.

Translation. A wily person does not walk on dry leaves (since they would betray his presence). This one describes the behaviour of deceitful people and their desire to remain out of the spotlight.

  • Karīki kamwe gatúkīrīirie ndutura kīrīmú.

Translation: darkness befalls an unwise dove who is out seeking one more grain of castor seed. This proverb is used to reprimand those who never seem to get enough and are always looking for more.

  • Múthiīrwo úgo-inī no úrīa arīrwo.

Translation: A person who sends another to the witch doctor has no choice but to believe everything he is told. This proverb targets those who do not believe in carrying out tasks themselves. By delegating to someone, one must accept the outcomes even if they are unpleasant.

  • Mútī útagútemwo ndúgerekagīrīrio ithanwa.

Translation: One should not aim an axe at a tree they do not intend to cut down. The proverb is told to people who are 'jacks of all trades but masters of none.

  • Njaú īrī ndoge nyina nī ndoge.

Translation: If a calf is poisoned, then so is its mother. This means that a child exhibiting bad behaviour often points to a problem originating from their parents.

Kikuyu sayings and their meanings

Besides the proverbs outlined above, the Kikuyu people also have numerous funny sayings, often with deep meanings. Here is a quick look.

  • Gwethera gītahī múka

Translation: To seek a wife for the stomach. This is one of the most common Kikuyu sayings and proverbs among people working to find something to eat. The search for food is equated to getting one's stomach a wife.

  • Kúrī gúkúa múrīo ta kīgwa.

Translation: Dying as a result of sweetness (like sugarcane). This saying is meant to warn people against the dangers of being too nice. It is the equivalent of 'he who makes himself a sheep shall be eaten by the wolf.'

  • Maitho ma arúme matiendaga kīndú kīhīnjú.

Translation: The eyes of men do not like to see anything thin. In traditional Kikuyu, the men considered fat children, wives, oxen and goats a sign of success. Their eyes were, therefore, said to despise anything that was thin.

  • Matarī makú mahītúkaga úgīkúnja itúma.

Translation: The water that does not belong to you will pass by as you fold your arrowroot leaf (traditionally used to drink water from the river). The saying means that whatever does not belong to you will pass by before you are ready.

  • Matienda mwako mendaga múrúgúrio.

Translation: They do not want to build, only to launch (the hut). This is used to reprimand those who never want to put in any work towards any project but want to celebrate the outcomes.

  • Kaihú gacangacangi gatigire kwao gúkīrugwo.

Translation: The wanderīng mongoose left home when a feast was being prepared. The saying is directed at people who never stick to one place and always give up just when something good is about to happen.

  • Nduma ndīhītithagīa múthii mbere.

Translation: Darkness never misleads anyone who travels before dusk. The saying means that humans are often the architects of their own disasters by failing to respect nature and other circumstances.

  • Ngīa na múigwa itikomaga.

Translation: The poor man and the thorn do not sleep. The former is kept awake by his worries, while the latter is always awake waiting for someone to injure.

  • Rútungu rwa gwītīnīrīa rútīrī gīthong’orī

Translation: The log that one chooses has no knot (imperfection). The saying means that anything that a person has chosen cannot have imperfections in their eyes.

  • Aka erī nī nyúngú igīrī cia úrogi

Translation: Two wives are like two pots of poison. The saying is directed to those who marry multiple wives and then place them in the same house. Such a man is said to be courting trouble.

There are numerous Kikuyu proverbs touching on various aspects of society. These proverbs were conventionally used to encourage virtues such as honesty and kindness and reprimand those with bad behaviour such as hate and gossip.
