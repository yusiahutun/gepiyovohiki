How much does it cost to import a car from Japan to Kenya?

Publish date: 2024-04-13

Japanese cars are increasingly becoming more popular in the Kenyan market. It is however important to verify that all vehicles imported into Kenya meet the least specification set by Kenya Bureau of standards. Cases in point of these standards include environmental and safety standards. The standard code of inspection, KS03 1515:2000 categorically spells out three main criterions used to accept an imported vehicle.

Total Cost of Importing a Car from Japan to Kenya

The cost of importing a car is relative and varies widely depending on a number of factors. Below are some of the factors that directly influence the cost of importing a car from Japan to Kenya.

Age Limit

Only vehicles with less than 8 years can be allowed for importation to Kenya. This duration is measured in years from the time the vehicle was first registered to the time of importation.

Left Hand Drive

Kenya does not allow any left hand drive vehicle for importation and registration except for approved purposes such as ambulances and government use. Kenyan road are solely designed for right hand drive vehicles.

Road worthiness

Any second hand vehicle imported to Kenya is first inspected for road worthiness. Other related inspections such as safety are equally performed before accepting the importation. Upon inspection, all Japanese vehicles are issued with a formal certificate indicating their road worthiness. This certificate is particularly issued by JEVIC and is required at the port when clearing with the custom requirements.

Port of Mombasa Customs Clearance

Before clearing an imported car from Japan, a licensed clearing and forwarding agent is engaged through an authorized access to Kenya customs Simba 2005 System used to clear cargo. Additionally, Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) Customs Services Department is actively involved in the process. Some of the information required for custom clearance include;

The cost of importing a car varied widely depending on the model of vehicle to be imported. There are other common determinants of import duty which in turn influence the Current Retail Selling Price of a specific vehicle right from the plant in which it was manufactured. Some of these include;

  • Vehicle make which include Toyota, Subaru, Mercedes, BMW, and Nissan among others.
  • Vehicle model which include Premio, Bluebird, Allion, Defender 110, and Forester.
  • Month and year of manufacture, and registration which varies from one vehicle to another
  • Engine capacity of the vehicle which determine fuel usage capacity; 1800 cc, 2000cc, 1500cc, and 1497cc among others.

Import duty is directly paid through Kenya Revenue Authority department of Customs Services. These arrears are deposited in Co-operative Bank of Kenya and National Bank of Kenya. Some of the common import duties payable when importing a vehicle from Japan include;

  • Import Duty –This account for 25% of customs value of the vehicle being imported. Import duty amounts to 25% of invoice value, Freight charges, and Insurance charges cumulatively.
  • Value Added Tax –This is accounts for 16% of the value attained by adding Excise duty, import duty and Customs value.
  • Excise duty –This accounts for 20% of the sum value of import duty and Customs value
  • IDF –Accounts for 2.25% of CIF value. IDF is payable at a minimum of KEs. 5,000

There are several calculators developed to help Kenyans intending to import vehicle to ascertain the cost and customs cost. These calculator indicate total expenditure to be incurred in the entire process. As a result, import duty calculators help importer to make informed choices before starting the importation process.

How to Import a Car from Japan to Kenya

Importers can opt to ship their cars in a container or Ro-RO. Most used cars are often shipped on RO-RO in which they are placed on the vessel deck. Shipping a car in a container is relatively expensive. 20 feet and 40 feet containers are used to carry several cars depending on their individual sizes. On average, it takes a week up to four weeks to maneuver its way from Japan to Kenya and southern Africa at large.

All second hand cars shipped to Africa are shipped in C&F or CIF basis. In practice many shipping firms do not ship inner cargo in cars being shipped to Africa. For instance, buyers who opt to buy spare parts for their car must ship them independently. Used car dealers in Japan carry out all the paper work involved in importation. These dealers prepare all vital importation documents including Insurance policy, Bill of landing, Vehicle export certificate, and invoices making it even easier for owners to receive their cars in time. The latter documents are mandatory for clearing a car at the port of Mombasa in Kenya.

Japanese Car Dealers

There are several domestic and exporter car dealers in Japan. Most of these dealers are based in Chiba, Kobe, Aichi, Osaka, Yokohama, Tokyo, and Nagoya among other prominent cities in the region. Many of these dealers buy used cars through auction and end user then export them overseas. These dealers have developed customized car importer and exporter platform to reach for more remote clients through trade cart view.

Import Duty Additional Costs

The process of hiring a container costs between KEs. 10, 000 and KEs. 13, 000. This cost must be paid to the shipping company before the actual transportation process begins.

  • Container freight station fees varies from KEs. 18, 500 to KEs. 35, 000 and is directly paid at the port. This cost is hugely influenced by the car model, make, and other related underlying factors.
  • Clearing and forwarding agents charges a fee of about KEs. 15, 000. C&F agents are helpful in tracking the shipment process.
  • Additional variant fee of KEs. 3, 000 at Mombasa Port car park
  • Cost may be incurred for possible minor repairs

It is important for the person importing a vehicle to be informed on all these requirement. Having all the knowledge about the process is necessary to avoid any suspicious dealing that may attract punitive fines that include seizure of the imported vehicle. Many Kenyans prefer to import used affordable cars. Used car importation accounts for over 80% of Japanese car importation to Kenya.
