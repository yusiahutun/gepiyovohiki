Guide to vision boarding | visualisation techniques for success

Publish date: 2024-02-12

While 2023 was the year of manifestation and ‘lucky girl’ syndrome, 2024 is all about vision boarding in order to achieve your goals, whether personal or professional. High-profile names such as Oprah Winfrey and Reese Witherspoon credit the visualisation technique for helping make their dreams become reality – in which case, the results speak for themselves.

But what exactly does it involve? Just as the name suggests, a vision board is all about painting a picture of the imagined success of your future self. Whether you're aiming for a new job, a loving relationship, or to master a particular skill, putting your intentions down on paper can be a highly motivating way to help you achieve it.

The actual vision board can look different for everyone – its form depends on your own personal preferences for retaining information. It might be a handwritten list of different goals or a collage of images that you find inspiring. It's not to be confused with a mood board, which acts more as a source of inspiration for a one-off project, rather than to help you achieve specific goals or ambitions you've set yourself.

To help you get started and build your own vision board for 2024 and beyond, we’ve enlisted the experts to share how they create theirs, the science behind the technique and how to make it work for you.

How does vision boarding actually work?

To put it most simply, it's a psychological technique. Creating a vision board helps to activate the brain's neural networks and is particularly effective for realising intentions because, if done properly, the brain interprets the board as a real experience, thanks to sensory perception, emotion and motor planning.

“Visualisation is considered a powerful tool for the brain, because it engages various cognitive processes and can have a significant impact on motivation, goal-setting, and overall mental wellbeing,” explains Dr Jenna Vyas-Lee, clinical psychologist and co-founder of mental healthcare clinic Kove. “While the term ‘vision boarding’ is often associated with a specific technique, the underlying principles draw on the science of visualisation and positive psychology. The bottom line is that visualisation, including vision boarding, can be a powerful tool to shape attitudes, beliefs, and behaviours, ultimately influencing the path to achieving personal and professional goals.”

Creating your vision board

Your vision board can look however you want; after all, it’s designed purely to help you achieve your personal goals. Set the mood by carving out plenty of time in your schedule to get immersed in the process. Many experts suggest lighting some candles or putting on some calming music – whatever you feel you need to be relaxed and comfortable.

“A vision board is a visual representation of what you want your life to look like,” notes Roxie Nafousi, the self-development coach and author of the bestselling Manifest: 7 Steps to Living Your Best Life. “It should have extremely specific and detailed goals on it. For this reason, my vision board only has writing on it, without any pictures at all. I just put down all my goals on a big sheet of card with the date I want to manifest them by at the top.”

If you find imagery works better, then start with the list and find material that represents the same emotion as the words you've used. “Sit down and write a list of all the things you’d like to have in your life in 2024,” notes Kirsty Gallagher, a lunar guide and spiritual mentor. “Then look for visual representations of these things in magazines, photographs or online. Keep an eye out for things that evoke emotions you want to feel in your life, or words that represent what you want your life to look like. I like to cut out and stick all these things together physically, but you can do vision boards digitally, too.”

In her book, Nafousi suggests that, before you start adding to your board, you take a moment to reflect on how you feel and really imagine who you want your future self to be in a few months, a year or even five years. Don't hold back; dream big and approach the process with no limitations. She then suggests splitting your life into six categories, and then writing down all the things you want to manifest within it:

  • Personal development (i.e. your personal growth/how you want to feel)
  • Love and romance
  • Career
  • Friends and family
  • House/home
  • Hobbies/leisure

How often do you update it?

Now that your vision has come together in one place – with your goals and aspirations clearly stated – what do you do next? Well, the beauty of vision boarding is you can add to it as you progress, but there's also no pressure to do so. "I personally don’t [add], but there are no rules," says Nafousi. "If you want to go back and cross something off and add something new, then you can do that. Or you can always make a new one; you could make a new 6-month vision board halfway through the year."

Gallagher adds: "It may be worth reviewing your board every quarter, solstice or equinox to make sure it's still aligned with who you are and where you want to go. We as humans should be constantly changing and evolving, so it’s OK that our dreams and goals change too."

But, she warns, resist temptation to change your board too often. “This can be a sneaky form of sabotage, as constantly changing the goalposts means you will never actually achieve anything. If you do find yourself doing this, check in as to whether there are any deep-rooted doubts or fears around having what you want."

When should I look at my vision board?

As the premise of a vision board is specific to you, its effectiveness varies from person to person. Depending on your preferences, daily routine or specific goals, there are benefits to looking at your vision board in the morning and evening – or, never at all.

"Some people find that looking at their vision board in the morning helps set a positive tone for the day," notes Dr Vyas-Lee. "The morning may be a motivating time to review goals and intentions, and visualising success at that time may result in a more optimistic outlook and better preparation for the challenges of the day ahead."

Agata Pospieszynska for Harper's Bazaar

That said, referencing your vision board in the evening holds just as many benefits. "Evening might be the perfect time for reflection and relaxation," adds Dr Vyas-Lee. "Reviewing before bed can offer a moment of introspection and allow individuals to unwind while focusing on their long-term goals. Some believe that the brain continues to process information during sleep, so viewing a vision board before bedtime may allow the subconscious mind to work on the visualised goals overnight."

Alternatively, it might help you to put your vision board away and not look at it all until the chosen date you wanted to achieve your goals by. This is Nafousi's personal preference and says that it's something she gets asked about a lot. "To manifest anything into your life you need to know what you want (be clear in your vision) and then you need to surrender to the journey that will lead you there," she writes in Manifest.

She acknowledges that other experts in the field suggest keeping your board somewhere prominent and checking it consistently, and that "the conflicting advice can be confusing". "Let me first say that there is a good argument for both sides and I am certainly not trying to say my way is the only way," she adds. "You should decide what feels best for you."

The best way to figure out what works for you is to try it out. "Ultimately, the most effective time to look at a vision board is the time that aligns with your personal preferences and daily routine," says Dr Vyas-Lee. "Experiment and see what you find most impactful."

What happens next?

Once you’re happy with your vision board, it’s important to always keep it at the forefront of your mind or somewhere you can easily access it (if you want to) when you feel you need a bit of guidance. But, perhaps most importantly, imagine that everything on there is already happening.

"Really imagine how your vision board would feel as a reality," says Gallagher. "Look at your board and visualise that these things have already happened. The purpose of a vision board is to help motivate and inspire you to go after what you want, so the more time you spend on it, the more you’ll feel it’s possible to make it your reality."
